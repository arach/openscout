# OpenScout Logo Exploration — § (Silcrow / Section Sign)

Status: draft exploration, not committed. Author: atelier-openscout-logo relay.

## Glyph brief

- Unicode U+00A7 SECTION SIGN. Also section mark, silcrow, signum sectionis.
- Read: a compact, typographic reference marker. Two interlocked S-curves around a vertical spine. Editorial / structural origin (not legal — legal use is borrowed).
- Shape cues: braided S, twin curves, central spine, interlock, routing/threading, section break.
- Risk surface: law-firm cliché, citation chrome, "academic paper" filing-cabinet vibe.

## OpenScout semantic anchors to map onto the glyph

- Routing: messages crossing between agents through a broker spine.
- Sections / snapshots: bounded slices of repo + fleet state.
- Threads: conversations, asks, replies.
- Handoff: one agent passing the baton to the next responsible owner.
- Observation: scout = looks ahead, marks where to read next.
- Structure: the mesh is organized, not a swarm.

The glyph already encodes most of this for free — two paths crossing a shared spine is a literal picture of agents trading turns over a broker. The work is to pull that meaning forward and push the "section of a legal brief" reading back.

## 12 directions

### 1. Silcrow Spine

- **Visual:** a tightened § with the vertical spine drawn heavier than the curves; the curves dock into the spine like cables into a bus.
- **Steals from §:** the literal interlock; the spine becomes an actual conduit.
- **Why OpenScout:** broker as a load-bearing column; agents (curves) clip on and off.
- **Risk:** still reads "section sign" if the curves stay too symmetrical.

### 2. Braided Route

- **Visual:** two open S-strokes interleaving without a spine, terminating in small filled dots top-left and bottom-right (source / sink).
- **Steals from §:** the over/under crossing pattern.
- **Why OpenScout:** routing diagram, two agents exchanging.
- **Risk:** ribbon / DNA / shoelace if the curve weight isn't disciplined.

### 3. Section Compass

- **Visual:** § rotated 90°; spine becomes a horizontal trail with two opposing arcs reading as headings off a track.
- **Steals from §:** the orthogonal posture, the marker semantics.
- **Why OpenScout:** scout reading the trail; route + bearings.
- **Risk:** can look like a stylized ampersand at small sizes.

### 4. Twin Helix (abstract)

- **Visual:** two strands crossing four times along a vertical axis, no closed loops, no serifs. 2D projection of a helix.
- **Steals from §:** the rhythm of crossings, not the silhouette.
- **Why OpenScout:** two-way thread, paired conversation, mesh-as-fabric.
- **Risk:** biotech / genome if rendered too organically — keep it engineered, equal-spaced.

### 5. Handoff Knot (abstract)

- **Visual:** two open C-arcs that touch at a single shared tangent point in the middle; no enclosed counters.
- **Steals from §:** the pivot at the waist of the §.
- **Why OpenScout:** baton pass, the exact moment ownership transfers.
- **Risk:** could read as a stylized infinity if the arcs are too symmetric — break the symmetry.

### 6. Channel Stack

- **Visual:** three short horizontal bars (a list of channels) with a small § sitting flush in the left margin as a section marker for the stack.
- **Steals from §:** its native editorial role — the marker beside content.
- **Why OpenScout:** channels as sections; the glyph indexes them.
- **Risk:** reads as a doc icon, not a logo. Best as an app icon, not a wordmark mark.

### 7. Mesh Node

- **Visual:** a small filled diamond node with two thin routing curves entering NW and exiting SE; their combined path traces a §.
- **Steals from §:** the silhouette, drawn as a network diagram.
- **Why OpenScout:** schematic, tool-like, says "broker" without writing it.
- **Risk:** looks like a generic node-graph icon if the § read isn't preserved.

### 8. Scout Marker (abstract)

- **Visual:** § with the top loop opened into an eye / aperture shape; the spine continues as a sight-line down through the bottom curve.
- **Steals from §:** the upper bowl, repurposed as a lens.
- **Why OpenScout:** scout = observation; the mark literally looks.
- **Risk:** anthropomorphic if the aperture gets pupil-like — keep it geometric.

### 9. Cut Section

- **Visual:** § rendered as an engraved / chamfered mark on a plate, with a hard 45° bevel on one side of every stroke. Monochrome.
- **Steals from §:** the silhouette; precision-engineered finish.
- **Why OpenScout:** tool-like, machined, "instrument" rather than "app."
- **Risk:** can drift into watchmaker / luxury-brand territory; keep the bevel restrained.

### 10. Section Slash

- **Visual:** a forward slash with a small S-curve absorbed into its top and bottom thirds, so the diagonal carries the § crossings.
- **Steals from §:** the crossings, mapped onto a slash.
- **Why OpenScout:** "/" is already in the product (paths, commands); reads as a route marker.
- **Risk:** at small sizes collapses into a slash and loses identity.

### 11. Threading Eye (abstract)

- **Visual:** a vertical seam with two opposing C-curves docking into it from left and right; together they form a closed eye-of-the-needle shape.
- **Steals from §:** the interlock and spine, abstracted into "thread through aperture."
- **Why OpenScout:** threading conversations through the broker; passing context.
- **Risk:** can read as a stylized lens / Cyclops eye — keep proportions narrow.

### 12. Open Section

- **Visual:** § where the upper loop is broken open into a routing tail that exits the frame to the right; lower loop is closed (origin).
- **Steals from §:** the silhouette, mid-transformation.
- **Why OpenScout:** "Open" Scout — the mark literally opens; narrative of handoff.
- **Risk:** asymmetric marks are harder to lock up; needs disciplined optical balance.

## Wordmark lockups

### L1. `Open§cout` inline

- § replaces the first `S` of Scout in a tight monospace / grotesk wordmark.
- The whole product name carries the mark; standalone § becomes an obvious favicon.
- Risk: reading puzzle the first time. Earned legibility after one exposure.

### L2. `OpenScout` + § corner mark

- Plain wordmark with a small § sitting top-left of the lockup as a section/citation marker for the product itself.
- Mark and wordmark are independent — § is reusable as an icon.
- Risk: leans editorial; works well in docs/UI chrome, less punchy as a launcher icon.

### L3. `Open § Scout` ligatured

- § as a separating glyph between Open and Scout, sized to match cap height, with consistent kerning so it reads as a single mark.
- The glyph does double duty — separator and emblem.
- Risk: depends entirely on a custom § that is sympathetic to the wordmark.

## Recommended to prototype

1. **#1 Silcrow Spine** + **L3 lockup** — keeps the source legible while reframing the spine as broker; cleanest path to a recognizable system mark.
2. **#5 Handoff Knot** + **L2 lockup** — the abstract pick. Sheds the legal reading entirely and earns the meaning back as "the handoff moment." Highest taste ceiling, highest design effort.
3. **#7 Mesh Node** + **L1 lockup** — tool-like, schematic, scales down to favicon and CLI badge without losing structure.

Backup: **#11 Threading Eye** if #5 reads too close to infinity in early sketches.

## Notes for the next owner

- Sketch #1, #5, #7 first at 24px, 64px, 256px before color. Anything that survives 24px monochrome earns a color pass.
- Avoid: gradient interiors, sparkle accents, isometric perspective, "AI" cyan/violet, terminal green. Target neutrals + one signal color.
- Test against the words "section," "marker," "thread," "spine," "handoff." If the mark reads as one of those, it's working. If it reads as "law firm," restart.
